Foundational Concepts & Definitions

Accounting · Cost Classification · Responsibility Centres · Cost Behaviour

Definition — Accounting
Accounting is the process of: Identifying → Recording → Classifying → Summarizing → Reporting → Interpreting business transactions and their effects on company affairs.

The Five Main Purposes of an Accounting System

#PurposeDescription
1Strategy & Long-range PlansNew product development, investments in tangible/intangible assets
2Resource AllocationProduct/customer emphasis, pricing, profitability analysis
3Cost Planning & ControlControl of operations and activities
4Performance MeasurementComparing actual vs. planned results; evaluation of people
5External ReportingMeeting regulatory and legal requirements (IFRS, IAS compliance)

Branches of Accounting

Financial Accounting

Serves internal and external decision-makers. Two phases: Book-keeping (day-to-day recording from source documents) and Summarising.

Management Accounting

Preparation of financial reports for use by people within the firm.

Cost Accounting

Part of Management Accounting. Establishes budgets, standard costs, actual costs. Analyzes variances, profitability, and social use of funds.

Definition — Cost
Cost = A sacrifice or giving up of economic resources for particular purposes, usually in exchange for goods or services.

Cost vs Expense: An expense is the value of assets given up to generate revenue. Most costs eventually become expenses.
Actual Cost = Historical cost incurred (distinguished from budgeted costs).

Objectives of Cost Accounting

a
Provide management with information to assist in formulating future plans covering all organizational functions.
b
Express the plan financially in terms of individual responsibility for all management grades.
c
Report on organizational effectiveness and methods used to carry out the plan.
d
Express standards of performance, compare actual performance with standards, and analyze variances for control.
e
Report to management on the extent to which the plan has been carried out.

Direct vs Indirect Costs

Direct Costs

Cost that can be traced in full to the product, service, or department. Easy to calculate; big enough to justify tracking individually.

  • Direct Materials — traceable material on a product (e.g. fabric for a tailor)
  • Direct Labour — remuneration of people directly involved in production (e.g. tailor's wages)
  • Direct Expenses — hire of special equipment for a specific job; maintenance of such equipment

Indirect Costs (Overheads)

Cost incurred in making a product but cannot be traced directly and in full. Cannot be economically identified with a specific saleable cost unit.

  • Indirect materials (cotton waste, grease, small tools)
  • Indirect labour (supervisors, cleaners)
  • Indirect expenses (rent, depreciation, insurance)

The Cost Structure — Key Formulas

Prime Cost = Direct Materials + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses Production Cost = Prime Cost + Production Overheads Total Cost = Production Cost + Selling & Administration Costs

Product Costs vs Period Costs

Product Cost

Cost identified with finished product; initially part of stock value on the balance sheet. Becomes an expense only when stock is sold.

= Inventoriable Cost (asset until sold)

Period Cost

Deducted as an expense during the current period. Never included in stock value.

= Selling & Admin costs, finance costs

Coding Systems

Definition
A code is a "brief, accurate reference designed to assist classification of items by facilitating entry, collation and analysis."

Two-Step Process

① Costs are classified → ② Costs are coded

Features of a Good Coding System

  • Easy to use and communicate
  • Each item has a unique code
  • Expandable for growth
  • Flexible — small changes easily accommodated
  • Comprehensive — every item can be coded
  • Brief — saves clerical time
  • Enables error detection
  • Index/reference book readily available
  • Regular review to remove outdated codes
  • Issued from a single central point
  • Consistent format — all numeric OR all alphabetic; no dots/dashes
  • Same length and structure (uniformity)
  • Avoids confusion (I/1, O/0, S/5)
  • Significant — code says something about the item

Overhead Costing

Allocation → Apportionment → Re-apportionment → Absorption

Definition
An overhead is a cost incurred in making a product, providing a service or running a department that cannot be traced directly and in full to the product, service, or department.

Components of Overhead

  • Indirect Materials – e.g. cleaning supplies, lubricants
  • Indirect Labour – e.g. supervisors, maintenance staff
  • Indirect Expenses – e.g. factory rent, insurance

Classification by Function

  • Production overheads – factory floor costs
  • Administration overheads – office running costs
  • Selling & distribution – delivery, marketing costs

Sources

Material requisitions · Wages analysis book · Vouchers & invoices

The 8-Step Overhead Costing Procedure

1
Establish Cost Centres
Identify Production cost centres (where cost units are made) and Service cost centres (support departments: stores, canteen, maintenance).
2
Collect Overheads
Gather all overhead costs from sources: wages book, invoices, requisitions.
3
Allocation
Charge whole cost items directly to a cost centre when that centre is the sole cause. E.g. indirect wages of Dept A go entirely to Dept A.
4
Apportionment (Primary)
Share general costs across cost centres on a fair basis (e.g. rent split by floor area).
5
Re-apportionment (Secondary)
Transfer service cost centre totals into production cost centres (because no cost units pass through service centres).
6
Calculate OAR
OAR = Budgeted Overhead ÷ Budgeted Activity Level
7
Absorb Overheads into Cost Units
Multiply OAR × actual activity (labour hours or machine hours) to charge overheads to products.
8
Calculate Over/Under Absorption
Compare overhead absorbed (OAR × actual activity) with actual overhead incurred.

Re-apportionment: 4 Methods Compared

Why re-apportion?
No cost units pass through service departments (Stores, Engineering), so their costs must be transferred to production departments.

① Direct Method

Ignores any inter-service dealings. Service centre costs split only between production departments.

Simplest

② Step-Down Method

One service centre is closed first (apportioned to all remaining), then the next. Order of closing affects results.

Moderate

③ Repetitive Method

Repeatedly cycles through service centres until remaining balances become negligible.

Iterative

④ Simultaneous Equations

Sets up algebraic equations recognising full inter-service dealings. Most accurate.

Most Precise
Re-apportionment — Direct Method

Stores re-apportioned using material requisitions ratio (Federal:International:Private = 30k:30k:20k → 3:3:2). Engineering using engineering hours (5k:3k:1k → 5:3:1).

Over / Under Absorption

Over Absorption

Overhead absorbed (OAR × actual activity) > actual overhead incurred.
→ Overcharged to products → Credit to P&L (profit increase)

Under Absorption

Overhead absorbed < actual overhead incurred.
→ Undercharged to products → Debit to P&L (profit decrease)

Over/Under Absorption = (OAR × Actual Activity) − Actual Overhead Incurred Positive = Over-absorption | Negative = Under-absorption

Standard Costing

Planned costs · Standard cost cards · Types of standards

Definition
Standard costing is the preparation of standard costs to value inventories/cost products and/or to use in variance analysis — a key management control tool. (CIMA)

Two Principal Uses

① Inventory Valuation

Alternative method to FIFO/LIFO for valuing inventories and costing production.

② Control Tool

Establishes planned costs, then uses variance analysis to highlight activities not conforming to plan — alerts management to areas needing corrective action.

Standard Cost Card

Shows the full planned cost breakdown per unit. Built from:

  • Standard material quantities × standard prices
  • Standard labour hours × standard rates
  • Standard variable overhead rates
  • Standard fixed overhead absorption rates

Types of Standards — At a Glance

TypeDescriptionMotivational Impact
IdealBased on perfect conditions — no waste, no idle time, no breakdowns❌ Unfavourable — goals feel unattainable
AttainableEfficient but not perfect — allows for some waste, fatigue, breakdowns✅ Best — useful psychological incentive
CurrentBased on current working conditions including present inefficiencies⚠️ No improvement incentive
BasicUnchanged over long periods — used to track efficiency trends⚠️ Least useful, may be outdated
LooseEasily achieved with little effort❌ Not challenging
HistoricalBased on last period's actual results❌ Not motivating — perpetuates past inefficiency

Problems in Setting Standards

  • Deciding how to incorporate inflation
  • Agreeing on performance standard (attainable vs ideal)
  • Deciding on quality of materials (cost vs wastage trade-off)
  • Estimating prices with seasonal variation or bulk discounts
  • Time required to construct accurate standards
  • Cost of setting up and maintaining the system
  • Behavioural problems — managers resisting for fear of blame

Variance Analysis

Material · Labour · Overhead · Sales variances

Key Definitions
Variance = Difference between a planned/standard cost and the actual cost incurred.
Adverse (A) = Actual worse than standard (costs higher OR revenue lower than planned).
Favourable (F) = Actual better than standard.

All Variance Formulas

Material Variances

Material Price Variance = (Standard Price − Actual Price) × Actual Qty purchased/used Material Usage Variance = (Standard Qty for actual output − Actual Qty) × Standard Price Material Total Variance = Price Variance + Usage Variance

Labour Variances

Labour Rate Variance = (Standard Rate − Actual Rate) × Actual Hours Paid Labour Efficiency Variance = (Standard Hours for actual output − Actual Hours Worked) × Standard Rate Labour Idle Time Variance = Idle Hours × Standard Rate [Always ADVERSE] Labour Total Variance = Rate + Efficiency (+ Idle Time) Variances

Variable Overhead Variances

Var OH Expenditure Variance = (Std Rate × Actual Hours) − Actual Variable OH Var OH Efficiency Variance = (Std Hours − Actual Active Hours) × Std Variable OH Rate Var OH Total Variance = (Std OH for actual output) − Actual Variable OH

Fixed Overhead Variances

Fixed OH Total Variance = (Std Fixed OH/unit × Actual units) − Actual Fixed OH Fixed OH Expenditure Variance = Budgeted Fixed OH − Actual Fixed OH Fixed OH Volume Variance = (Actual units − Budgeted units) × Std Fixed OH/unit Fixed OH Efficiency Variance = (Std Hours − Actual Hours) × Budgeted Fixed OH Rate [= (SH−AH) × BFOH] Fixed OH Capacity Variance = (Actual Hours − Budgeted Hours) × BFOH Rate [= (AH−BH) × BFOH]

Sales Variances

Sales Price Variance = (Actual SP − Standard SP) × Actual units sold Sales Volume Variance = (Actual units sold − Budgeted units) × Standard contribution/gross margin

Inter-relationships Between Variances

When two variances are interdependent, one is usually adverse and the other favourable. Never analyse a variance in isolation.

Materials Price & Usage

Cheaper material → favourable price but adverse usage (more waste). Expensive material → adverse price but favourable usage.

Labour Rate & Efficiency

Skilled (expensive) workers → adverse rate but favourable efficiency. Cheap inexperienced workers → favourable rate but adverse efficiency.

Sales Price & Volume

Price cut → adverse price variance but favourable volume. Price rise → favourable price but adverse volume.

Cost & Sales Volume

Cheap materials → adverse sales volume (customers avoid lower quality). Better quality → adverse cost variances but favourable sales volume.

CVP Analysis

Breakeven · Contribution · P/V Ratio · Margin of Safety

Definition
CVP Analysis is the study of the interrelationships between costs, volume and profit at various levels of activity. It helps answer: What volume to break even? What volume to earn a target profit? How do price changes affect profits?

Contribution

Contribution = Sales − Variable Costs

Contribution per unit = SP − Variable cost per unit. When contribution > fixed costs → profit.

Breakeven Point

BEP (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution per unit BEP (value) = BEP units × Selling Price

Target Profit

Units needed = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) ÷ Contribution per unit

Profit/Volume (P/V) Ratio — Also called C/S Ratio

P/V Ratio = (Contribution ÷ Sales) × 100 Also: Change in Contribution / Change in Sales × 100 Or: Change in Profit / Change in Sales × 100 → Total Contribution = P/V Ratio × Sales → Profit = (P/V Ratio × Sales) − Fixed Costs → Fixed Costs = (P/V Ratio × Sales) − Profit → BEP in value = Fixed Costs ÷ P/V Ratio

CVP Assumptions

  • Costs and revenues are linear throughout the relevant range
  • All costs classified accurately as fixed or variable
  • Selling price constant at all levels of output
  • Variable cost constant per unit
  • Fixed costs constant at all output levels
  • Units produced = units sold (no closing inventory)
  • Constant sales mix (for multi-product companies)
  • Only volume causes cost changes

Limitations of CVP Analysis

  • Only applies to a single product or fixed mix of products
  • Breakeven charts can be time-consuming to prepare
  • Assumes fixed costs are constant at all output levels
  • Assumes variable costs are the same per unit always
  • Assumes sales prices are constant at all levels
  • Ignores uncertainty in cost estimates
  • Ignores inventory level changes
Wimble Company — CVP Full Solution

Data: Variable cost K20,000/unit · Fixed costs K50,000,000/year · Selling price K70,000/unit

Contribution per unit = K70,000 − K20,000 = K50,000 (1) BEP in Units = K50,000,000 ÷ K50,000 = 1,000 units BEP in Value = 1,000 × K70,000 = K70,000,000 (2) Units to earn K30,000,000 profit: = (K50,000,000 + K30,000,000) ÷ K50,000 = K80,000,000 ÷ 50,000 = 1,600 units (3) If 400 more units (2,000 total) are made with additional fixed costs of K16,000,000: New fixed costs = 50,000,000 + 16,000,000 = K66,000,000 Required contribution to earn same profit (K30,000,000) from 2,000 units: Contribution needed = 66,000,000 + 30,000,000 = K96,000,000 Contribution per unit = 96,000,000 ÷ 2,000 = K48,000 New Selling Price = K20,000 + K48,000 = K68,000 per unit

Costing Methods

Job · Contract · Process costing

Specific Order Costing

Job Costing — each customer order is a separate identifiable unit. Short duration work in a workshop/factory.
Batch Costing — similar to job costing but for a batch of identical items.
Contract Costing — like job costing but large scale, long duration (construction, civil engineering).

Continuous Operational Costing

Process Costing — continuous flow, indistinguishable units (oil refining, soap, paint, food manufacture).
Service Costing — used for service industries where output is intangible.

Contract Costing — Key Features

  • Formal contract between customer and supplier
  • Customers' special requirements
  • Relatively long duration
  • Constructional in nature
  • Work is based on site
  • Most costs are direct costs
  • Potential for progress payments & retentions
  • Profit taken on incomplete contracts

Taking Profit on Incomplete Contracts

Degree of CompletionProfit Recognition Rule
Less than 30%❌ No profit taken — outcome too uncertain
30% to 80%Profit = 2/3 × Notional Profit × (Cash received ÷ Value of work certified)
Above 80%Profit = (Progress payments ÷ Contract price) × Estimated total profit
OR (Value certified ÷ Contract price) × Total profit [if no retention]
Expected LOSS⚠️ Full expected loss recognised immediately (prudence concept)
Degree of Completion = (Costs to date ÷ Total estimated costs) × 100 Notional Profit = Value of work certified − Cost of work certified Progress Payment Due = Value certified − Retention (10%) − Payments already made

Process Costing — 4-Step Framework

1
Determine Output and Losses
Calculate expected output. Identify actual output. Determine normal loss, abnormal loss, or abnormal gain. Calculate equivalent units if there is closing WIP.
2
Calculate Cost per Unit
Cost per equivalent unit = Total costs ÷ Expected (good) output
Normal loss is given no share of cost.
3
Calculate Total Cost of Output, Losses and WIP
Normal loss cost = 0. Abnormal loss/gain valued at same rate as good output. WIP uses equivalent units.
4
Complete the Process Account
DR side: inputs (materials, labour, overheads). CR side: normal loss, output (finished goods), abnormal loss. Write up abnormal loss/gain account.

Types of Loss in Process

Normal Loss

Expected loss — budgeted percentage of input. Given no cost; its cost is absorbed by good units.

Abnormal Loss

Loss in excess of normal loss. Valued at same rate as good output. Written off to income statement via Abnormal Loss Account.

Abnormal Gain

Less loss than expected. Valued at good output rate. Debit in process account; credit to income statement via Abnormal Gain Account.

Opening WIP Methods

FIFO Method

First-in-first-out. Closing WIP valued at current period costs. Opening WIP costs carried forward separately and attached to completed units.

Weighted Average Method

Opening WIP values added to current costs to get an overall average. Both closing WIP and completed units use the same average cost.

Process Costing — Abnormal Loss & Gain Example

Input: 1,000 units @ K4,500. Normal loss: 10% (100 units). No opening/closing WIP.

Expected output = 1,000 − 100 = 900 units Cost per unit = K4,500 ÷ 900 = K5 per unit Scenario A: Actual output = 860 units (actual loss = 140) Abnormal loss = 140 − 100 = 40 units × K5 = K200 Scenario B: Actual output = 920 units (actual loss = 80) Abnormal gain = 100 − 80 = 20 units × K5 = K100
